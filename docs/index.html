A lens for software delivery

Unrealized Knowledge

The cost of not-yet-knowing — and why, now that code is cheap, it is the whole game.

What just changed

For most of software's history, the expensive part was making it. Turning an intention into working code took so long, and cost so much, that the entire discipline organized itself around production: how to estimate it, schedule it, staff it, speed it up. Every methodology war of the last fifty years was, underneath, an argument about how to manage production cost.

That cost just collapsed. Generating a working implementation of a well-described intention is becoming cheap — cheap enough that arguing about it is starting to feel like arguing about the price of compilation.

But when a bottleneck gets cheap, the constraint doesn't disappear. It moves. It moves to the thing production was always waiting on: knowing, precisely, what is worth building and what is true about the space you're building in. Code got cheap. Intent stayed expensive. The discipline's center of gravity is shifting from the first to the second, and most of our processes, metrics, and instincts haven't caught up.

A name for the gap

Unrealized Knowledge (URK) is everything a finished system will eventually have forced you to know that you have not yet captured. Not knowledge that's missing from the universe — knowledge that exists somewhere (in a stakeholder's head, in production behavior, in what a legacy API actually does as opposed to what its documentation says) but has not yet been extracted into a form anyone can build against.

It comes in two kinds, and they behave differently:

Business Unrealized Knowledge — what would make this worth doing. Who it's for, what changes for them, and what counts as success. It approaches zero when someone can say, before the work ships, "here is how we will know we're done."

Tech Unrealized Knowledge — what is true and uncertain about the implementation space. What the existing system actually does, which constraints are real, what will break when you touch it.

Every project starts with both kinds high. Delivery is what happens when both approach zero. That's the reframe this lens proposes: delivery isn't the act of producing code. Producing code is a side effect of having extracted the knowledge. If you could somehow extract all of it without writing a line, the writing would be a formality — which, increasingly, it is.

How the two curves fall

The two curves don't behave the same way, and the difference matters.

Business URK should fall early and stay down. If nobody can articulate what success looks like by the time design starts, everything downstream is speculation with a schedule attached.

Tech URK falls non-monotonically. It spikes. A clarifying question surfaces a dependency nobody mentioned. A design review reveals the architecture doesn't permit the easy path. Implementation discovers what the documentation lied about. These spikes are not failures of the process — they are the moments discovery actually happens. The spike was always there, latent; the process just made it visible. The only real question is when you can afford to have it. A spike during design costs a conversation. The same spike during a production rollout costs an incident.

Two payment plans for the same debt

Through this lens, the great methodology argument dissolves into something less dramatic. Waterfall and agile look like opposites. They are two payment plans for the same debt — and the debt is Unrealized Knowledge, the cost of not-yet-knowing.

Waterfall tries to pay the whole debt upfront: extract all the knowledge, then build. It fails where knowledge genuinely doesn't exist yet at specification time — you cannot know in a requirements meeting what only implementation can reveal, so the plan quietly accrues interest it never priced in. Agile pays in installments: extract a little, build a little, let the building itself do the extracting. It fails differently — the installments can go on forever, and nothing forces the business curve to zero.

Neither changes the amount owed. They change when you find out. As long as iteration was expensive, that timing question was the most important one in software, and we fought over it for decades. Now that generating a working attempt costs little, iteration timing stops being the interesting variable. The binding constraint becomes something both camps always took for granted: how precisely you can articulate what you want, and how rigorously you can verify that what you got matches it.

Shift left — as far as the cycle allows

If Unrealized Knowledge is the cost, the strategy writes itself: extract it as early in the cycle as you can. Every piece of knowledge realized at the whiteboard is a spike that never happens in production.

But the honest version of the rule has a boundary condition, and it's doing all the work: extract knowledge as early as the cycle allows — not "as early as possible." Two things bound it. Knowledge availability: some knowledge does not exist yet at the earlier phase; no amount of upfront analysis can realize what only contact with the real system reveals. Capture cost: some knowledge is cheaper to discover by building a cheap attempt than by holding another meeting about it — and cheap generation has made this truer, not less true. Shift-left is an optimization under constraints, not a commandment. Pretending otherwise is how you reinvent waterfall with better tooling.

What it changes about measurement

Tests check that the code matches the contract. Tests do not check that the contract was worth writing.

All of our verification machinery — tests, reviews, type systems, CI — verifies downstream of intent. It can tell you the system does what the specification says. It is structurally silent on whether the specification captured what mattered. That silence was tolerable when writing code was the dominant cost, because the specification got years of forced contact with reality as humans slowly built against it. When implementation compresses from months to hours, that contact disappears, and specification error ships at machine speed.

So the lens suggests measuring processes by a different question. Not "how much did this phase produce?" but: how much Unrealized Knowledge did this phase extract, on which axis, and could it have been extracted earlier at reasonable cost? A requirements document that surfaces a fatal constraint is worth more than one that's merely complete. A design review that triggers a spike did its job precisely because it hurt. A sprint that ships nothing but eliminates a wrong path may be the most productive week of the quarter — and output metrics will record it as zero.

A lens, not a methodology

Unrealized Knowledge doesn't tell you which rituals to run or which artifacts to produce. It's a lens: one question you can hold up to any phase, meeting, or document in any process you already have. What knowledge did this realize? Which axis? And could it have been realized earlier, at a cost worth paying?

Ask it a few times and the shape of your own delivery cycle becomes visible — where knowledge actually gets extracted, where it merely gets scheduled for later, and where the spikes are quietly waiting for the most expensive possible moment to appear.
